Australia's beaches are an icon of national identity, but underneath the surf and sunshine, two datasets tell a richer story. This visualisation draws on over 200 years of shark incidents from the Australian Shark Incident Database, and the 2025 water quality grades of every monitored NSW swim site from the Beachwatch program. Together they reveal where, when, and how Australia's ocean edge is both beautiful and unforgiving.

Section 01 — Geography

Where does the

Shark incidents cluster along Australia's eastern seaboard, not because sharks are more prevalent there, but because people are. NSW and Queensland together account for over 67% of all recorded incidents. Hover over any point to explore the year, species, activity, and outcome.

Section 02 — Time

The rise that isn't
what it seems

Incident counts have climbed sharply since 1980, but Australia's coastal population tripled over the same period and water sports participation exploded. The risk per person in the water has remained relatively stable. There are simply far more people swimming.

Section 03 — States

A tale
through coastlines

NSW leads in total incidents, a product of population density and surf culture. But Western Australia tells a different story: fewer incidents overall, yet a fatality rate nearly twice the national average. Remote surf breaks bring people face-to-face with white sharks far from emergency services.

Section 04 — Species & Activity

Three sharks,
one pattern

White, tiger, and bull sharks account for the overwhelming majority of serious incidents. But what you're doing in the water matters as much as which shark is nearby. Surfers and swimmers dominate incident counts simply because they are the most common activities, and spend the most time in the water.

Why do surfers dominate the count? A surfboard viewed from below can resemble the silhouette of a seal, the white shark's preferred prey. Surfers also spend hours in the mid-break zone near channel exits where predatory sharks patrol. This doesn't mean surfing is uniquely dangerous, it means surfers are the largest group in the distant waters.

Section 05 — Seasonality & Behaviour

Summer peaks and
the provocation divide

January is the single most incident-prone month, correlating directly with peak beach visitation across Australia. The provoked vs unprovoked split reveals a critical insight: provoking a shark (through fishing, handling, or spearfishing) dramatically changes the outcome profile.

Section 06 — Water Quality

How clean are
beaches really?

The NSW Beachwatch program grades every monitored swim site annually based on enterococci bacterial contamination. The 2025 dataset covers 249 sites across all of NSW: from Pacific Ocean beaches to harbour bays, coastal lagoons, and freshwater swimming holes. Where you swim matters enormously.

What causes poor grades? Rainfall is the primary driver. Stormwater runoff carries bacteria from animal waste, sewage overflows, and urban surfaces into swim sites. Enclosed waters, harbours, estuaries, lagoons, etc. are most vulnerable because they don't flush quickly like ocean beaches. After heavy rain: wait at least 1 day at ocean beaches and 3 days at enclosed water bodies before swimming.

Section 07 — What the Data Tells Us

Swim smart,
not scared

Two datasets, 232 years apart in origin, converge on the same message: Australia's beaches are remarkably safe, but knowing the data helps you make better decisions about where and when to swim.

07

🦈 Shark Risk

Fewer than 15 unprovoked incidents per year on average nationally. NSW has the most; WA the highest fatality rate. Avoid dawn and dusk. Never handle or provoke a shark.

💧 Water Quality

73% of NSW swim sites graded Good or Very Good in 2025. Avoid enclosed water bodies after heavy rain. Check beachwatch.nsw.gov.au before swimming — forecasts update twice daily.

📍 Best Bets

Ocean beaches consistently outperform enclosed water bodies on water quality. The Hunter Coast and Illawarra regions have the strongest track records. Avoid swimming near stormwater drains anywhere in NSW.
